Effect of posture on blood pressure

Study how the blood pressure, heart rate and other parameters change when there is a change in posture. Perform experiments like HUTT (Head UP Tilt Test) and analyze the results.


OVERVIEW

This module can be used to study the effect of change in posture on blood pressure. The Head-up-tilt test can be performed by controlling the tilt angle (in degrees) and the time taken (in seconds) for the tilt table to reach the angle.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES

1. Understand how gravity affects the blood pressure

2. Study the effect of varying gravitational field multiple arteries (Upper body and lower body)

3. Simulate and study the effect of microgravity on blood pressure

INTRODUCTION

A tilt table test (sometimes known as a passive head-­up tilt test (HUTT) is used to record blood pressure and heart rate each minute, while the patient is tilted on a table at varying levels. The average duration of the test is about an hour. The HUT test is often used to assess patients ability to regulate blood pressure, in particular for patients who suffer from frequent episodes of syncope, light- headedness, or dizziness . During the test, the patient first rests on a tilt-table in supine position. After steady values for pressure and heart rate (HR) have been established, the table is tilted to an angle which is customizable. Upon tilting, gravity pools approximately 500–1000ml of blood in the lower extremities reducing venous return, which induces a reduction in cardiac filling, pressure and volume. In response, blood pressure in the upper body is decreased, while blood pressure in the lower body is increased. [1]

For normal subject, this pressure imbalance causes the baroreflex to kick in causing increase in heart rate, cardiac contractility, and vascular resistance and decrease in vascular compliance. Whereas in subjects with autonomic dysfunction this is associated with severe risks including dizziness, loss of balance leading to falls, and reflex mediated syncope, in particular, for the elderly and for patients with hypertension and diabetes.[1]

MATHEMATICAL MODEL

The model for the simulation of Head-up-tilt test (HUTT) has been adopted from a lumped parameter model that has been developed by T.Heldt.[3]As described by Sam Ahmad Zamanian [2], the hydrostatic pressure gradient imposed on a tube (under a constant accelerational field of gravity) by tilting it upwards is given by

D * g * h * sin(a)

where,
D - density of the fluid (blood)
g - gravitational accleration
h - effective height of the artery
a - angle of tilt

The effective height h of the compartment is assumed to be one half of the anatomical vertical heights; the exception to this is in the legs, where the effective height is one third of the anatomical height.

CARDIAC REGULATION

Any aberrant changes in blood pressure would be automatically compensated by the body by changing other parameters such as heart rate, resistances and so on. Baroreflex is one such system that kicks in when there is any aberration detected. There are two baroreceptors located in the body, one in the carotid sinus and the other in the aortic arch. The baroreflex mechanism is a fast response to changes in blood pressure.
